The Future of Defence

What New Technologies Have Emerged?

TARDEC’s (Tank Automotive Research Development and Engineering Center) autonomous trucks. As the name implies, these trucks can work on their own and deliver supplies safely, wherever it may need to be. These vehicles have many different functions and features that they can use while in the field, such as remote-operation.

HUGIN, an AUV (Autonomous Underwater Vehicle), mainly used by militaries for reconnaissance, surveillance, and intelligence. It has three different input modes that allow for different functions, such as being fully autonomous, partially autonomous, and supervised, meaning that it provides real-time data while on a mission.

Predator Drone, a Remotely Piloted Aircraft System (RPAS), an offensive weapon used during the War on Terrorism, which was terrifyingly effective, to both enemies and civilians. While these weren’t fully autonomous, they can easily be made pretty close to being just that, and considering the civilian casualties it has caused, who knows the damage it could have caused if it really were automotive?

The Questions

In the end, any autonomous machine does not make decisions like a human can, it only executes decisions already made for them. However, just like humans, they aren’t perfect either. An offensive machine like a predator drone, even if made to only attack the enemy, can still harm civilians. This is a complex issue that can only be answered by breaking down the problem. It creates even more questions that we need to answer.

There are generally three different roles a machine can play in the military, offensive, defensive, and support. Figuring out what specific technologies should and shouldn’t exist is the first step to figuring out whether they should exist at all.

On the topic of offensive technologies, they shouldn’t exist at all. They are most capable of causing harm to civilian life, and shouldn’t be deployed against human enemies. It’s simply unethical to do so, because machines have no regard for the lives of those enemies they encounter. A regular, human soldier, is capable of making decisions that take responsibility for the enemy, which a machine simply cannot do. A machine being deployed into war is like using chemical weapons, it’s not something you can so easily stop, and it simply isn’t fair to use against humans.

Now defensive technologies, as the name suggests, are meant for defense, they are meant to protect something from an incoming attack force. These should have the option to be fully automated when protecting the lives of other humans, but for the most part, should only be partially automated.

Support Technologies cannot attack or defend, so they mainly include anything that can perform reconnaissance, intelligence gathering, and supply runs. Since they do not cause any direct harm, they should be fully autonomous. They do what their human counterparts already do, but even better. While the information they can collect can cause harm, so can the humans, and the information that they can collect. In the end, the information they collect, should only be interpreted and used by humans.

This is a really difficult question to answer, the employment of certain technologies can eliminate the loss of human life by a significant amount, but it can also completely remove the people who still want to serve and make a difference. In the end, these technologies should be used scarcely, only when absolutely necessary. In terms of the specifics, automated defenses should be used only when absolutely necessary, while support roles can be used quite often, since most of the time, it is definitely necessary.

Conclusion

Yes, these technologies should be used, but only in the specific ways mentioned above. They should be constantly monitored, and even partially controlled at times. That way, the loss of human life is reduced greatly, while also allowing for humans to still serve important roles in the military.
